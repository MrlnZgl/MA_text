\section{Plane section verification}
The following section is employed as a reliability test of the governed results for the curved \acrshort{tbl} simulations.
As described in XX, we detected pressure fluctuations in the snapshots during the course of the simulations.
To ensure that these fluctuations leave the long-term statistics unaffected, we verified the statistics of the curved \acrshort{tbl} simulations in the straight inlet.
In order to undertake this task,  we compare the statistics of case R100deg30 in optimised and base configurations with the ones from the plane \acrfull{tbl} setup and the reference data for plane \acrshort{tbl} simulations from XX, which has previously been introduced.
The statistics are taken at $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta} = 700$ in all simulations performed. This corresponds to a position of around $s_{Re_{\theta}= 700} = \frac{1}{2} s_p$.
Consequently,  the impact of the curvature should be negligible at this position, and all setups should  demonstrate analogous behaviour.
Following this, the results of this comparison are presented for case R100deg30. 
In order to circumvent the presentation of repetitive results, analogous analyses for the remaining curved configurations are not shown here.

\section{Curved turbulent boundary layer results}

In the following section the results of the curved \acrshort{tbl} simulations are discussed.
Firstly, the results of each tested case are presented in isolation to directly compare the optimised configuration and the base configuration.
Then, the optimised configurations of all cases are compared to study the effect of different geometrical configurations on the optimisation and the development of the \acrshort{tbl}.


\subsection{R100deg 30}
First, the results of case R100deg30 are presented. 
This case is analysed using  multiple quantities to ensure a proper evolution of the \acrshort{tbl} along the curvature.
We initially focus on the first- and second-order statistics extracted at a value of  $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta} = 1200$. 
This position is located within the curvature, with  a certain distance to both ends. This is done to mitigate interfering effects of the geometry changes.
Then, the development of the boundary layer thickness and Reynolds numbers in the streamwise direction is presented. 
Here, we use as reference data from plane \acrshort{tbl} simulations performed by \citet{jimenez_near-wall_2013} extracted at $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta} = 1100$.
Finally, we will discuss the pressure-oriented parameters to evaluate the optimisation capability.

In \autoref{fig:R100deg30Re1200U} the velocity profile in viscous scales is depicted.
It is evident that the base and optimised configuration exhibit a complete agreement in their velocity profiles.
Both also match the reference profile.
As already discussed in the previously shown velocity profiles, the only difference at the upper end of the boundary layer is attributable to  the different extraction positions and the varying values of $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta}$. 
In addition, it can be seen that the profiles align with  the analytical solutions of the \benennung{linear law} and the \benennung{log law}.
This indicates a correct transformation of the velocity components from the fixed $xyz$-coordinate system into streamwise and wall-normal components. 
As the components in \name{neko} are defined within a fixed coordinate system, all quantities are subject to transformation during the post-processing stage.
The correct implementation can be justified by the velocity profiles since, in transformed directions, the qualitative shape in the wall-normal direction should not change in the curvature.
In \autoref{fig:R100deg30Re1200Fluct} the Reynolds stress components are depicted.
As with the velocity profiles, the differences between the optimised and the base configuration are negligible.
When compared to the reference data, only minor discrepancies  in the outer region of the \acrshort{tbl} are visible.
To analyse the global evolution of the \acrshort{tbl}, the normalised boundary layer and momentum thicknesses are displayed in \autoref{fig:R100deg30Re1200Thickness}.
Their growth pattern is clearly impacted by the curvature.
Up to a value of $s/\delta_0 \approx 55$, both quantities exhibit a consistent increase in their respective growth patterns as the streamwise length is increased.
Then, a sudden decline of both is observed. 
This position coincides with the start of the curvature in the domain, indicating that the geometry change impacts the boundary layer growth.
After a brief period in the streamwise direction, the profiles recover and increase again. 
However, both quantities exhibit a reduced slope such that their growth is decelerated in the curvature compared to the straight section.
This phenomenon has been identified and previously documented  by XX.
In the curvature, the growth rate remains constant until the end of the curvature is reached.
A sudden change in both quantities is evident at $s/\delta_0 \approx 110$ which marks the end of the curvature.
Reversely to the start of the curvature, here a sudden increase of the growth rate can be detected across all quantities.
Similarly, this region is quite short in the streamwise direction until the slope is aligned at a comparable level to that observed  in the curvature.
This observation lends support to the hypothesis proposed by XX, which states that the curvature exerts an influence on the growth of a boundary layer.
Besides this general behaviour exhibited by both configurations, some local differences between them can be identified.
In the straight segment, the configurations perfectly match each other. 
Right before the curvature starts, a minute negative protuberance manifests  for the optimised configuration.
The subsequent decrease in thickness measurements is analogous across the various configurations.
After the recovery of the decrease, the profiles start to diverge.
It can be seen that for both measures, namely $\delta_{99}$ and $\theta$, the optimised profile develops a higher growth rate in comparison to the base profile.

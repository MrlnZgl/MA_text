\section{Channel simulations}\label{sec:channel}
At the start of this project, channel simulations were performed. 
These reproducible simulations were executed to to familiarise ourselves  with the \acrshort{dns} code and the effect of different settings. 
In addition, the created post-processing script was verified using these  and reference results.
The \name{neko} installation includes some examples, one of which is for a turbulent channel simulation.
We used this example as a starting point and made some modifications\fxinfo{klingt vage: besser: and modified it according to our needs (geht das genauer?) }.

\subsection{Simulation setup}

The simulation is set up for a turbulent channel flow  with a Reynolds number of  $Re_\tau = 180$.
With a bulk velocity $U_b = 1$ and a channel half height $\delta = 1$, the bulk Reynolds number is set to be $Re_b = 2800$.
The simulation runs with a polynomial of order 7 and a time order of 3.
The physical domain size is $ L_x \times L_y \times L_z = 4\pi \delta \times 2 \delta \times \frac{4}{3}\pi \delta$.
We set \benennung{no-slip}
at the top and the bottom walls, as \acrshort{bc} which forces $u$ and $v$ to be 0 at the walls.
Physically, this  corresponds to a flow whose viscosity causes the streamwise velocity component to be equal to its limiting wall value, which is $=0$ in this case. \fxchange{Ist es möglich 1.2 Sätze hinzuzufügen, warum das sinnvoll ist?}
The wall-normal component is restricted by the impermeable characteristics of the walls.
The streamwise and spanwise walls are set to have periodic \acrlong{bc}, meaning that  anything leaving one side enters the domain at the opposite side.
Consequently, turbulence motions evolving along the channel length are recycled to accelerate  the process of turbulence evolution.
In order to set up the channel simulation, we apply some \fxinfo{the following} modifications to the example case from the \name{neko} documentation.
First, we refine\fxinfo{alles PRäsenz oder alles Past} the mesh.


This is necessary to ensure a proper resolution of all turbulent scales.
The mean flow and  statistics only vary  in the wall-normal direction, since the periodic \acrshort{bc} in the streamwise and spanwise directions render the flow statistically homogeneous.
In contrast, the instantaneous velocity field is three-dimensional, with the steepest gradients in the wall-normal direction close to the wall.
Therefore, a fine resolution in the wall-normal direction is required close to the wall.
As a well-established reference,   the first grid point is positioned at $y^+<1$ from the wall, with a fine wall-normal resolution at the wall being used
 \citep{schlatter_assessment_2010}.

The mesh is coarsened towards the channel centre since the turbulent scales grow with increasing wall distance.
In the streamwise and spanwise directions, a uniform spacing is used. 
%
As the flow is statistically homogeneous in both directions, the smallest scales are independent of the wall-parallel position.
As the turbulent structures elongate in the streamwise direction, a higher resolution is required in the spanwise direction  to properly resolve the short dimension of the structures.
We define the grid spacing as $\Delta z^+= 6$, an established size according to \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}.

In the streamwise direction, it is a common practice  a spanwise resolution of twice the value used in the other directions, i.e. a value of $\Delta x^+= 12$ (XXQUELLE). 
Next, we apply the new \acrshort{ic} to the velocity field.
A laminar velocity field following the laminar profile $u = 1.5(1-y^2)$ is applied as a basis.
Then, a set of analytically prescribed perturbations is designed to trigger rapid transition to turbulence.
These perturbations consist of combinations of polynomial and sinusoidal functions.
Five perturbation modes are applied overall, with different wave numbers corresponding to different scales in the flow.
All perturbations are constructed in a manner that they satisfy the \benennung{divergence-free criterion} by construction.
The new setup of \acrshort{ic} is depicted in \autoref{fig:channel_IC}.
To constantly drive the flow, a \benennung{flow rate force} is applied. 
\fxinfo{XX info über flow rate force}
Simulations are performed with this setup.

Consequently,  the mean value and as well the fluctuations are highest in this direction.
The components of the $\langle v'v'\rangle ^+$ and $\langle w'w'\rangle ^+$ tensors have their peaks at much smaller values.

Since the velocity components in non-streamwise directions are much smaller, their fluctuations also must be much smaller compared to the streamwise fluctuations.
The only component showing negative values is $\langle u'v'\rangle ^+$  .
This behaviour is based on the momentum of the particles causing these fluctuations.
Particles with $v' > 0$ move away from the wall, which at the same time comes along with a smaller streamwise velocity. This means that the streamwise fluctuation $u'$ is also  smaller than the fluctuations of the surrounding particles in the new occasion \fxchange{?? situation?}.
This leads to a negative sign in the Reynolds stress.
With a particle moving the opposite direction $v$, the explanation can be reversed.
This particle has a negative $v'$, moving towards the wall and causing  fluctuation overshoot in the streamwise direction, as it is arriving from a faster region. \fxinfo{XXQUELLE}
Therefore, this configuration also results in a negative shear stress component.

As distance from the wall increases, both terms decrease.

The production is balanced in this region by the viscous diffusion, dissipation and the turbulent transport.

\section{Plane turbulent boundary layer}\label{sec:planeTBL}

The following section presents the simulations performed on a plane  \acrshort{tbl}.
First, the simulation setup is described, followed by a discussion of the results.


\subsection{Simulation setup}

In the next part of the project we create a setup for simulating a \acrshort{tbl} over a plane wall.
These simulations are used to verify the general settings for \acrshort{tbl} simulations, and to validate the results through by comparing them with those of similar simulations.
In comparison with the channel simulations, the flow characteristics are different in a \acrshort{tbl} which requires a  different simulation setup.
Unlike a channel simulation, the flow of a \acrshort{tbl} is statistically inhomogeneous in the streamwise direction since the \acrshort{tbl} grows in this direction.
As a consequence, separate conditions for the inflow and the outflow must be defined, which will be discussed in detail in the next paragraph.
These properties impact the selection of an appropriate domain size.
The streamwise length is chosen based on two requirements.
First, the domain  must be long enough for  the development of the \acrshort{tbl} to become independent of the prescribed inflow condition \fxinfo{XXQUELLE}.
Secondly,  it must be long enough to enable the development of the \acrshort{tbl} up to the desired $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta}$.
The wall-normal dimension is chosen so that the \acrshort{tbl} is unaffected by the upper wall and the free stream remains undisturbed. 
In the spanwise direction the dimension is selected so that the largest turbulent structures remain unaffected by the applied periodic \acrlong{bc}.
To define the dimensions properly, we used $Re_{\theta} = 1000$ as a reference value which the \acrshort{tbl} should reach within the plane domain.
The streamwise position at which this value is reached is marked as $x_0$ with a corresponding \acrshort{tbl} thickness of $\delta_0$.
Based on this, we set the dimensions to $L_x \times L_y \times L_z = 53\delta_{0} \times 4\delta_{0} \times 4\delta_{0}$, corresponding to $325 \times 24.5 \times 24.5$ in physical units.
The next step is to create a mesh for this domain.
It should be noted that the growth of the \acrshort{tbl} causes variations of the viscous scales.
This is  due to the reduction of $u_{tau}$ downstream, which leads to an increase of $\delta_{\nu} = \nu u_{\tau}$.
With a constant physical $\Delta x$, this implies that $\Delta x^+ = \Delta x/\delta_{\nu}$ decreases downstream.
So the resolution requirement is most restrictive at the upstream end of the domain.
Therefore, $u_{\tau}$ at the reference position $s_0$ is used to derive the viscous dimensions since this coincides with the upstream end of the domain. \fxinfo{nicht so wirklich aber nacja, liegt bei 85prozent der domain länge}
The resolutions are adopted from the channel setup (see \autoref{sec:channel}).
This is valid since, as stated in \autoref{sec:turbulenceTheory}, the turbulent structures in the near-wall region are independent of the Reynolds number and the flow geometry.
The streamwise and spanwise resolutions  are adopted directly from the channel simulation ($x^+= 12$ and $z^+ = 6$). 
In the wall-normal direction, a slightly higher resolution is used, with the first grid point staged  at $y^+ = 0.8$.
The mesh is coarsened in the wall-normal direction up to a height of $2\delta_0$.
The coarse resolution is then fixed up to $L_y - \delta_0/2$.
The small bend between this slice and the upper wall has an increased resolution, since  the shear stress is measured there and requires a higher resolution.
The mesh is created using  \name{gmsh}. 
First, a 2D mesh is constructed in the $x-y$/ streamwise-wall-normal-plane \fxinfo{oder: streamwise, wall-normal} is created. 
Using the mesh transform tool in \name{neko}, it is then extended in the spanwise direction.

\subsubsection{Boundary and initial conditions}

To setup the \acrlong{tbl} boundary and initla conditions are necessary. 
In the initial condition a boundary layer is imposed at the lower wall. 
Since a laminar boundary layer has the quite easy blasuis profile as solution, a laminar boundary layer is given as initial velocity condition. 
The velocity in the free-stream $U_0$ is set to be 1 initially.
AS boundary conditions we impose a no-slip condition on the lower wall.
The outflow has the boundary condition 'outlfow+dong', which means XXX.
For the upper wall we tested two different setups.
For one simulation we put a 'normal outflow' condition at the top wall, which allows outflow in normal direction across the domain end.
In the otehr simulation we imposed a Dirichlet condition with (1,0,0) as velocity profile. 
This is done to compare the effect of the different boundary conditions on the flow behaviour. 
This becomes especially importnat for the simulation of the curved \acrshort{tbl}, since there the boundary condition needs to be imposed piecewise on the top wall.

It can be seen that all three profiles follow the analytical definitions of the linear law and the log law.
The reference profile differs from the two performed simulations in the region of the outer flow, which can be explained through the lower $Re_{\theta}$ of the reference data.
Besides from that, no differences between the profiles can be observed.
For the fluctuations similar observation holds.
In the viscous sublayer the profiles coincide for all fluctuation components.
In the wake region it can be seen that the reference values are slightly below the two performed simulation data, which again be explained with the lower $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta}$. \\
The presented results show a proper agreement of both simulations with the reference data.
Based on that two conclusions can be drawn.
First, the simulations seem to generally create reasonable results compared to other \acrshort{tbl} simulations.
And second, the difference between the two simulation with different boundary conditions at the top wall are negligible small.

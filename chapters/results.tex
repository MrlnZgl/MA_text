\section{Plane section verification}
The following section works as a reliability test of the governed results for the curved \acrshort{tbl} simulations.
As described in XX we detected pressure flcutuations in the snapshots during the simulations.
To ensure that these flcutuations leave the long-term statistics unaffected we verified the statistics of the curved \acrshort{tbl} simulations in the straight inlet.
In order to do this we compare the statistics of case R100deg30 in optimised and base configuration with the ones from the plane \acrfull{tbl} setup and the already introduced reference data for plane \acrshort{tbl} simulations from XX.
The statistics are taken at $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta} = 700$ in all performed simulations, which corresponds to a position of around $s_{Re_{\theta}= 700} = \frac{1}{2} s_p$.
Therefore, the impact of the curvature should be negligible at this position and all setups should show similar behaviour.
Following, the results of this comparison are presented for case R100deg30. 
In order to circumvent the presentation of repetitive results, analogous analysis for the remaining curved configurations are not shown here.

The first and second order statistics of the compared simulations are depicted in \autoref{fig:inflowSecVerif} (a) - (c).
The streamwise velocity profile in viscous scaling is shown in \autoref{fig:planeTBLverifU}.
It can be seen, that all profile match each other. 
As already discussed in \autoref{subsec:planeTBLResults} only the free-stream velocity of the reference data is slightly below the other profiles since they are reported at a lower value of $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta}$.
Therefore, the boundary layer thickness is smaller which corresponds to a faster achievement of the free-stream velocity.
In \autoref{fig:inflowSecVerifFluct} the Reynolds tress are visualised. 
We can observe a good fit of the performed simulations for all components.
Again, the reference data show a small difference in the outer region of the boundary layer, respectively higher $y^+$ regions.
This phenomenon can also be implied by the lower Reynolds number.
The budget of the \acrshort{tke}, visualised in \autoref{fig:inflowSecVerifBudget}, give the same impression.
In this metrics, the three profiles were found to be highly consistent with one another and the reference data.
The statistics analysed so far demonstrate no noticeable differences between the plane and the curved \acrshort{tbl} simulations including both, the base and the optimised simulation setup of the R100deg30 case.
The last discussed quantity is the Clauser parameter $\beta$, represented in \autoref{fig:inflowSecVerifBeta}.
It is shown here only in the straight region of the domains, since this is the region of interest for this verification.
The streamwise coordinate is normalised by the reference boundary layer thickness $\delta_0$.
The trend of all simulations looks similar, meaning $\beta$ is constant near zero, except a small balancing region at the beginning of the domain.
Along the streamwise direction a slightly growing distance between the plane setup and the curved setups can be observed ending in a maximum distance at the end of the straight section of around $0.1$.
This trend could already show an impact of the curvature even in the upstream region.
However, all profiles show quite smooth trends of $\beta$ indicating low to negligible impact of the pressure fluctuations on the mean Clauser parameter.
Overall, the comparisons obtained show that in the straight region the simulations end up in similar profiles which leads to two mean conclusions.
First, it generally verifies the curved simulation setup in enabling an undisturbed \acrshort{tbl} development in the straight section.
And second, the results imply that the observed pressure fluctuations, discussed in XX show now visible manipulation of the statistics.



\section{Curved turbulent boundary layer results}

In the following section the results of the curved \acrshort{tbl} simulations are discussed.
First the results of each tested case are presented separately to directly compare the optimised configuration with the base configuration.
Then, the optimised configurations of all cases are compared to study the effect of different geometrical configurations on the optimisation and the development of the \acrshort{tbl}.

\subsection{R100deg 30}
First, the results of case R100deg30 are presented. 
This case is analysed with multiple quantities, to ensure a proper evolution of the \acrshort{tbl} along the curvature.
In the beginning we focus on the first and second order statistics extracted at $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta} = 1200$. 
This position is located inside the curvature, with some distance to both ends, to avoid interfering effects of the geometry changes.
Then, the growth of the boundary layer thickness and Reynolds numbers along the streamwise direction are presented. 
Here, we use as reference data from plane \acrshort{tbl} simulations performed by \citet{jimenez_near-wall_2013} extracted at $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta} = 1100$.
Finally, we will discuss the pressure-oriented parameters to evaluate the optimisation capability.

In \autoref{fig:R100deg30Re1200U} the velocity profile in viscous scales is depicted.
It can be seen that the base and optimised configuration show no difference in their velocity profiles.
Both also match the reference profile.
As already discussed in the previous shown velocity profiles, the only difference at the upper end of the boundary layer occurs because of the different extraction positions, respectively varying values of $\mathrm{Re}_{\theta}$. 
In addition, it can be seen that the profiles follow the analytical solutions of the \benennung{linear law} and the \benennung{log law}.
This indicates a correct transformation of the velocity components from fix $xyz$-coordinate system into streamwise and wall-normal components. 
Since the components in \name{neko} are defined in a fixed coordinate system, all quantities are transformed in the post-processing.
The correct implementation can be justified by the velocity profiles since in transformed directions the qualitative shape in wall-normal direction should not change in the curvature.
In \autoref{fig:R100deg30Re1200Fluct} the Reynolds stress components are depicted.
Similar to the velocity profiles, the differences between the optimised and the base configuration are negligible.
Also compared to the reference data only small variations in the outer region of the \acrshort{tbl} are visible.
To analyse the global evolution of the \acrshort{tbl}, the normalised boundary layer and momentum thicknesses are displayed in \autoref{fig:R100deg30Re1200Thickness}.
Their growth is clearly impacted by the curvature.
Up to $s/\delta_0 \approx 55$ both quantities constantly grow with increasing streamwise length.
Then, a sudden drop of both is remarkable. 
This position coincides with the start of the curvature in the domain which implies that the geometry change impacts the boundary layer growth.
After a small period in streamwise direction, the profiles recover and increase again. 
However, both quantities show a reduced slope such that their growth is decelerated in the curvature compared to the straight section.
This phenomenon is known and was already detected by XX.
in the curvature the growth rate stays constant until the end of the curvature is reached.
A sudden change of both quantities is remarkable at $s/\delta_0 \approx 110$ which marks the end of the curvature.
Reversely to the start of the curvature, here a sudden increase of the growth rate can be detected in all quantities.
Similar, this region is quite short in streamwise direction until the slope levels at a comparable niveau as in the curvature.
This observation forces the statement of XX that the curvature effects the growth of a boundary layer.
Besides this general behaviour for both configurations, some local differences between them can be detected.
In the straight part the configurations perfectly match each other. 
Right before the curvature starts, a small negative bump is visible for the optimised configuration.
The following drop of the thickness measures is similar in the configurations.
After the recovery of the drop, the profiles start to differ.
It can be seen that for both measures $\delta_{99}$ and $\theta$ the optimised profile develops with a higher growth rate than the base profile.

Assumptions about the reasons for this behaviour are discussed later, once the impact general of the optimisation on the flow evolution is discussed through pressure analysis.
In the optimised configuration the change of the growth rate due to the end of the curvature is smaller compared to the based profile.
However, the impact of the curvature start seems to be more downstream, since the slope change starts earlier.

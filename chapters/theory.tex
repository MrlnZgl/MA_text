In the following section a short introduction in the theory of wall-bounded turbulence is given.
As a detailed discussion of this would be beyond the scope of this work, interested readers are thus directed to consult \citet{pope_turbulent_2015,moin_fundamentals_2024}.
The notations employed in this work are not expounded in detail but rather are based the work of \citet{pope_turbulent_2015}. \\
The description of fluids is based on three physical principles:
The first one  states that a fluid can be treated as a continuum \citep{batchelor_introduction_2000}.
Second, we take the viscous property of fluids into account, i.e.~a Newtonian fluid relates shear stresses to the rate of strain \citep{tennekes_first_1972}
Finally, it is assumed that the law of mass conservation holds.
Using this, two important equations can be derived.
The \benennung{continuity equation} for viscous incompressible flows is given 
as


\begin{gather}
    \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial (\rho u_i)}{\partial x_i} = \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_i} = 0  
\end{gather}

$u_i$ represents the velocity components in the three spatial directions $x_i$.
The density $\rho$ is assumed to be constant for incompressible flows.
The second relevant equation describes the momentum conservation of a fluid.
These so-called \benennung{\acrfull{nse}} can be written as 

\begin{gather}
    \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial t} + u_j \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} = -\frac{1}{\rho} \frac{\partial p}{\partial x_i} + \nu \frac{\partial^2 u_i}{\partial x_j \partial x_j} \\[4pt]
\end{gather}

with $p$ representing the pressure and $\nu$ the dynamic viscosity of the fluid.
These equations fully describe the motion of a fluid, since they are applicable for most kind of flows \citep{batchelor_introduction_2000}.
Building on this, the behaviour of fluids is often split into three groups with different characteristic properties:
These categories are defined as \emph{laminar},  \emph{turbulent} and  \emph{transitional}. 
If a flow acts in the laminar regime its streamlines do not cross and the flow is time-independent \citep{davidson_turbulence_2015}.
Disturbances begin to develop in the transitional regime, causing disruptions in the laminar behaviour.
In the turbulent regime the behaviour of the flow is chaotic and time-dependent \citep{schlichting_boundary-layer_2017}.
It shows irregular time- and location-dependent random fluctuations \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}.
Consequently, in turbulent flows enhanced mixing in the fluid can be observed \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}.
To describe the behaviour of a flow in terms of laminar or turbulent behaviour the \benennung{Reynolds number} is introduced as 
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Re} = \frac{U L}{\nu}
\end{equation}
where $U$ and $L$ are reasonable velocity and length scales, and $\nu$ the viscosity of the fluid.
Flows with low Reynolds numbers show laminar flow behaviour.
With an increasing Reynolds number the fluid arrives first in the transitional region between laminar and turbulent until it reaches a fully turbulent state.
At high Reynolds numbers one can observe a separation of scales with different behaviours \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}.
One can split the turbulent motions into large- and small-scale motions.
The large scales are mostly controlled by the outer constraints of the flow, e.g., its geometry and the boundary conditions.
The behaviour of the small-scale motions, however, depends on the viscosity and the receiving energy from the large scales \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}, which makes their behaviour universal and independent of the specific flow case.
This universal behaviour can be measured with statistics over a long time averaging.

The most turbulent flows are bounded by at least one solid wall \citep{moin_fundamentals_2024}.
They are decomposed into \benennung{external} and \benennung{internal} flows. 
Internal flows are limited by outer walls like pipes or channels.
External flows refer to flows which occur around a solid body like an airfoil or a wall.
A characteristic feature of external flows is that the flow sufficiently from the body remains unaffected by its \citep{moin_fundamentals_2024}.
The flow behaviour in the immediate vicinity of the wall is in most cases very similar due to the universal nature of small scales turbulences \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}.
In the following section we will introduce a set of definitions to describe this near-wall behaviour.
is remarkably similar due to the universal nature of the near-wall small-scale turbulence \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}.

\subsection{Turbulent boundary layer theory} \label{subsec:TBLTheory}

Boundary layers occur when fluids with non-zero viscosity stream over a stationary wall. 
In these setups, the so called \benennung{no-slip} boundary condition affects the flow behaviour significantly.
The \benennung{no-slip} boundary condition  asserts that the velocity components of the fluid in the wall-normal and streamwise directions must be zero directly at the wall since there is no relative movement between the wall and the fluid \citep{schlichting_boundary-layer_2017}. 
The velocity sufficiently distant from the wall, known as the \benennung{free-stream velocity} $U_0$, is usually much higher than zero. This observation indicates the presence of a transition region where an acceleration of the fluid occurs \citep{prandtl_uber_1904}.
This so-called \benennung{boundary layer} is placed close to the wall. 
The thickness of the boundary layer is known to vary depending on the Reynolds number. In general, the boundary layer decreases with increasing Reynolds number \cite{schlichting_boundary-layer_2017}. 
The flow in the boundary layer may behave laminar or turbulent. In this thesis we will focus on \acrfull{tbl}.

The boundary layer grows in the streamwise direction. It can be described via multiple characteristic quantities. 
The introduction of these quantities requires a consistent coordinate system: 
The streamwise direction  is denoted by  $x$  with the corresponding velocity $u$. The wall-normal direction is denoted by $y$ and the associated velocity  $v$.  $z$ denotes the spanwise direction and is associated with the  velocity component $w$.
In the free stream, distant from the wall, we assume $U_0$ being the dominant velocity component. 
The streamwise velocity component inside the boundary layer is denoted  $u(x,y)$.
Often, the thickness of the boundary layer is modelled using three different parameters \citep{schlichting_boundary-layer_2017}: 
the \emph{boundary-layer thickness} $\delta_{99}(x)$ describing the distance from the wall at which the velocity $u(x,y)$ derives $99\%$ of the free-stream velocity;
the \emph{displacement thickness} $\delta^*$, which defines the displacement of an inviscid fluid to preserve the same mass flow;
and the \emph{momentum thickness} denoted by  $\theta$  is employed to define the displacement of an inviscid fluid in order to fulfil the conservation of momentum.
The following equations can be used to derive these quantities:
\begin{gather}\label{eq: tblThickness}
    u (x, \delta_{99}(x)) = 0.99 \cdot U_0 \\[1ex]
    \delta^*(x) = \int_0^\infty \left( 1 - \frac{u(x,y)}{U_0} \right) \mathrm{d}y \hspace{0.8cm}
     \theta(x) = \int_0^\infty \frac{u(x,y)}{U_0} \left( 1 - \frac{u(x,y)}{U_0} \right) \mathrm{d}y
\end{gather}


In this thesis we use the momentum thickness $\theta$ to characterise the height of the boundary layer.
This approach is adopted from \citet{schlatter_assessment_2010} since it facilitates a  more precise measurement in comparison to the boundary-layer thickness. 
%To describe the boundary-layer with the Reynolds number, 
The boundary-layer is related to the Reynolds number, 
with different Reynolds numbers being defined based on the introduced displacement measures:

\begin{gather}
    \mathrm{Re}_x = \frac{U_0 x}{\nu}, \quad
    \mathrm{Re}_{\delta_{99}} = \frac{U_0 \delta_{99}}{\nu}, \quad
    \mathrm{Re}_{\delta^*} = \frac{U_0 \delta^*}{\nu}, \quad
    \mathrm{Re}_\theta = \frac{U_0 \theta}{\nu}. \\
    \nu : \text{viscosity} \nonumber
\end{gather}

In order to measure velocities in a turbulent flow the velocities are decomposed in a time averaged component denoted by $\langle \rangle$ and fluctuations denoted by  $u_i'$. 

\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:ReynoldDecomposition}
    u_i = \langle u_i \rangle + u_i' = U_i + u_i' \text{ with } \langle u_i'
\rangle = 0\end{equation}

Equation \ref{eq:ReynoldDecomposition} represents the so-called \benennung{Reynolds decomposition}.
Applying it on the \acrshort{nse} leads to the \benennung{\acrfull{rans}}\citep{reynolds_iv_1895}. 
For the boundary layer the \acrshort{rans} equations is reduced to 

\begin{gather}
    0 = \nu\,\frac{\mathrm{d}^2 U}{\mathrm{d}y^2} - \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}y}\langle u'v'\rangle - \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial \langle p\rangle}{\partial x} \\
    0 = -\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}y}\langle v'v'\rangle - \frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial \langle p\rangle}{\partial y} 
\end{gather}

By means of rearrangement and the imposition of boundary conditions, it is possible to derive the total shear stress, denoted by $\tau$, and its derivative as follows:

\begin{gather}
    \tau = \rho\nu\,\frac{\mathrm{d} U}{\mathrm{d}y} - \rho\langle u'v'\rangle \quad
    \frac{\mathrm{d}\tau}{\mathrm{d}y} = \frac{\mathrm{d}p_{\mathrm{w}}}{\mathrm{d}x}.
\end{gather}


By applying  the no-slip boundary condition the shear stress at the wall is derived, which then results in
\begin{equation}
    \tau_w = \mu \left. \frac{\mathrm{d}U}{\mathrm{d}y} \right|_w 
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Non-dimensionalisation}

In order to undertake a study of the phenomena present in a boundary layer, it is first necessary to define the so-called viscous scales, since the ranges of dimensions and velocity variations are much smaller than in the free stream \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}.
%
Based on the wall shear stress $\tau_w$, $\rho$ and $\delta_{99}$, the friction velocity $u_{\tau}$, the viscous length scale $\delta_{\nu}$ and the friction Reynolds number $\mathrm{Re}_{\tau}$ are introduced as follows:
\begin{gather}
    u_{\tau} = \sqrt{\frac{\tau_w}{\rho}} \hspace{1.0cm}
    \delta_{\nu} = \frac{\nu}{u_{\tau}} \hspace{1.0cm}
    \mathrm{Re}_{\tau} = \frac{\delta_{99} u_\tau}{\nu}
\end{gather}

With these quantities, the original length and velocity scales can be non-dimensionalised in wall units, also called plus units, as shown in \citep{moin_fundamentals_2024}:
\begin{equation}
    y^+ = \frac{y}{\delta_v} = \frac{y u_\tau}{\nu}, \qquad
    u^+ = \frac{U}{u_\tau}.
\end{equation}

The merits of these non-dimensional scales are twofold.  
Firstly, they are universal for all wall-bounded flows enabling the comparison between flows of different physical dimensions \citep{von_karman_mechanische_1930}.
Moreover, the non-dimensionalisation facilitates a detailed investigation of the velocity profile inside the \acrshort{tbl}.
\citet{prandtl_uber_1904} observed that the \acrshort{tbl} contains an inner part where the mean velocity profiles are independent of the free-stream quantities $U_0$ and $\delta_{99}$ such that the mean velocity profile can be fully described by the plus units.
In the region closest to the wall – the \benennung{viscous sublayer} – $U^+$ is a linear function of $y^+$. 
With increasing wall distance, the velocity profile transitions such that it follows a logarithmic function.
This so-called \benennung{log law} is defined as 
\begin{gather}
    U^+ = \frac{1}{\kappa}\ln y^+ + B \\
    \text{with } \kappa = 0.41 \quad B = 5.2
\end{gather}

The values estimated for the constants $\kappa$ and $B$ vary throughout the literature.
In the context of this work the values introduced by \citet{pope_turbulent_2015} are used as reference.

As  introduced,  the application of the Reynolds decomposition on the \acrshort{nse} result in the \acrshort{rans}.
From this equation, a single term known as the \benennung{Reynolds stresses} can be extracted.
For turbulent flows, the Reynolds stresses are significant characteristics, since the transport of mean momentum is based on them \citep{moin_fundamentals_2024}. 
The tensor is defined as 
\begin{equation}
    R_{ij} =  \langle u_i' u_j'\rangle.
\end{equation}
The inner part $u_i' u_j'$ of the tensor elements $R_{ij}$ represents the product of the velocity fluctuations.
To determine the Reynolds stresses, the mean $\langle . \rangle$  is taken.
Although the mean of a single fluctuation equals zero, the product of two fluctuation components is not necessarily zero. 
The diagonal entries contain the normal stresses $\langle u' u'\rangle, \langle v' v'\rangle, \langle w' w'\rangle$.
The remaining entries contain the shear stresses.
According to \citep{schlichting_boundary-layer_2017}, the only non-zero entry is $\langle u' v'\rangle$.
The issue with this tensor is that direct computation is not possible.
%
Computing the pressure and the three velocity components is based on  four equations, namely the continuity equation and the Navier-Stokes equation in three dimensions.

The Reynolds stress tensor is accompanied by six additional terms that have to be resolved.
This issue is referred to as the \benennung{closure problem} \citep{wilcox_turbulence_2006}.
%
In order to resolve this issue, a number of different approaches can be adopted  to derive additional equations that will ensure that all terms can be computed. These approaches are described in detail in fundamental books like \citet{wilcox_turbulence_2006,pope_turbulent_2015,moin_fundamentals_2024}.
The transport equations for the Reynolds stresses can be determined by taking the appropriate moments of the \acrshort{nse} \citep{mansour_reynolds-stress_1988}.
For incompressible flows the non-dimensionalised transport equations can be stated as 
\begin{gather}

    \begin{aligned}
        \mathcal{P}_{ij} &\colon \text{production rate,} & \quad \varepsilon_{ij} &\colon \text{dissipation rate,} \\
        T_{ij} &\colon \text{turbulent transport rate,} & \quad D_{ij} &\colon \text{viscous diffusion rate,} \\
        \Pi_{ij} &\colon \text{velocity pressure-gradient term,} & \quad \frac{\overline{\mathrm{D}}}{\overline{\mathrm{D}}t} \langle u_i u_j \rangle &\colon \text{convection}
    \end{aligned} \notag
\end{gather}

\subsection{Clauser parameter in turbulent boundary layers}

In boundary layers the free-stream pressure gradient plays a crucial role since it has a significant impact on the flow behaviour \citep{clauser_turbulent_1954}.
From the \acrshort{nse} one can derive that the pressure for a \acrshort{tbl} is constant along a flat wall in the wall-normal direction.
In the streamwise direction, however, it does not necessarily have to be constant.
Bernoulli's equation establishes a correlation between the free-stream pressure and the free-stream velocity. Consequently, the gradient in the streamwise direction can be expressed as follows:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bernoulli}
    -\frac{\mathrm{d}p_0}{\mathrm{d}x} = \rho\, U_0 \frac{\mathrm{d}U_0}{\mathrm{d}x}.
\end{equation}
This equation shows that an acceleration of the flow results in a negative pressure gradient, which is also referred to as \benennung{\acrfull{fpg}}. 
In the other hand, an increase of the velocity yields a positive or \benennung{\acrfull{apg}}. 
It can thus be concluded that a \acrshort{tbl} with a constant free-stream velocity will have no pressure gradient.
This configuration, designated as \benennung{\acrfull{zpg}}, has been the subject of extensive research due to its status as the natural configuration in a plane \acrshort{tbl} \citep{spalart_direct_2024,schlatter_assessment_2010}.
However, it is possible to apply boundary conditions such that an adverse or favourable pressure gradient results.
To measure the pressure gradient, a commonly used quantity is introduced,  the so-called \benennung{Clauser parameter $\beta(s)$}, which is defined as follows:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:beta}
    \beta(s) = \frac{\delta^*(s)}{\tau_w(s)} \frac{\mathrm{d}p_w}{\mathrm{d}s},
\end{equation}

This non-dimensionalised parameter defines the ratio between the streamwise pressure force on the boundary layer and the wall shear stress \citep{clauser_turbulent_1954}.
In the literature, this parameter is widely used to categorise the flow according to the introduced pressure regimes:

\begin{equation}
    \beta =
    \begin{cases}
        < 0, & \text{favourable pressure gradient (FPG)}, \\
        = 0, & \text{zero pressure gradient (ZPG)}, \\
        > 0, & \text{adverse pressure gradient (APG)}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}




\section{Curved turbulent boundary layers}

The majority of research conducted on \acrshort{tbl} has focused on the utilisation of \acrshort{tbl}  in contexts of straight walls \citep{pargal_adverse-pressure-gradient_2022}. 
However, in many real-world scenarios, the wall may not be completely straight, but rather exhibit curved segments \citep{spalart_direct_2024}. 
The effects in \acrshort{tbl} along curved walls are still poorly studied, since the problem involves a range of inherent complications \citep{appelbaum_systematic_2025}. 
Due to the curved shape, a pressure gradient in streamwise direction builds up naturally which makes it hard to separate the source of observed physical effects \citep{so_experimental_nodate}. 
As \citet{so_experiment_1973} observed in their experiments without pressure adjustment, the pressure decreases slowly towards the bginning of the curvature and then decreases fast once the curvature is reached. 
Since these pressure variations have a significant impact on the turbulent structures, an indepth study of curvature effects necessitates the  compensation of these pressure variations \citep{appelbaum_systematic_2025}. 
Therefore, it is necessary to study curved \acrshort{tbl} under zero-pressure gradient conditions \citep{so_experimental_nodate,gillis_turbulent_1983}, or to impose a defined pressure gradient \citep{schwarz_convex_1996,pargal_adverse-pressure-gradient_2022,spalart_direct_2024}. 
In experimental studies, the achievement of a defined pressure distribution is facilitated by adjusting the position of the upper wall \citep{so_experimental_nodate, johnston_experimental_nodate, gillis_turbulent_1983,schwarz_convex_1996}. 

From the  studies mentioned before, some general statements regarding the effects of curvature on the \acrshort{tbl} may be  deduced.
First, a boundary layer along a convex wall with  \acrshort{zpg} shows a smaller growth rate compared to a straight developing \acrfull{tbl} \citep{so_experimental_nodate}, \citep{meroney_turbulent_1975}. 
This phenomenon can be attributed to a pressure gradient in the centripetal direction. 
It balances the centrifugal force of the fluid elements. 
The application of  centrifugal force  will result in a displacement of fluid particles with high velocities to a new outer position. 
The pressure gradient forces the fluid particle in the opposite direction \citep{so_experimental_nodate}, \citep{meroney_turbulent_1975}.
\citet{gillis_turbulent_1983} observed a decrease in the turbulent scales as consequence of a convex curvature. 
They also found that the wall law for the curved wall corresponds to that observed in measurements taken on a flat wall, using the same parameters. 

Besides experimental investigations, some numerical studies about the characteristics of curved \acrshort{tbl} have been presented.
In a similar manner to the experimental investigations, a modification of the setup is necessary to compensate for the streamwise pressure gradient \citet{appelbaum_systematic_2025}. 
One possible approach for achieving this  is to setup  both a curved and a flat \acrshort{tbl} simulation model and match their pressure distributions.
The naturally occurring pressure gradient in the curved domain is then imposed as boundary condition on the flat \acrshort{tbl} simulation.
With the flat plate as reference case it is, therefore, possible to isolate the effects of curvature.
This approach is utilised  by \citet{spalart_direct_2024,pargal_adverse-pressure-gradient_2022}.
A similar approach was adopted by \citet{bobke_history_2017} in their study on the effect of history on \acrshort{apg} \acrshort{tbl}.
They applied the distribution of the Clauser parameter $\beta(x)$ from a wing \acrshort{dns} on a flat \acrshort{tbl} through the imposition of corresponding velocity profiles along the top wall.
They studied various distributions and showed that even with matching $\beta(x)$ and $Re_{\tau}$ the geometry of the wall alters the flow \citep{bobke_history_2017}.
Another possibility, of course, is the study of curved setups without manipulation of the pressure as done by \citet{vinuesa_pressure-gradient_2017,tanarro_effect_2020}.
\citet{vinuesa_pressure-gradient_2017} analysed data from flows along a wing which were derived by \citet{hosseini_direct_2016}.
They investigated the occurring pressure field and observed that even minor variations of $\beta$ altered
 the development of the boundary layer when compared to baseline configuration \acrshort{zpg} \acrshort{tbl} \citet{vinuesa_pressure-gradient_2017}.
\citet{tanarro_effect_2020} extended these studies and analysed the different effects of local and integrated pressure gradient distributions.
However, these studies mainly focus on the effect of pressure on the flow characteristics referring to the argument that the pressure-driven effects superpose the curvature effects \citep{patel_longitudinal_1997}.
\citet{yang_assessment_2016} studied a  setup that was quite similar  to the one used in the project, with a view  to investigating curvature effects in \acrshort{tbl}. 
Their domain has a straight inlet, then a 60° circular bend section followed by a straight tail.
The experimental results of the \acrshort{rans} demonstrated that, in comparison with \acrshort{dns} results, the accuracy of the  turbulence models tested was inadequate in cases involving large curvature \citep{yang_assessment_2016}.
In the study of \citep{appelbaum_systematic_2025} a concave \acrshort{tbl} was investigated. 
In order to achieve a \acrshort{zpg} distribution the pressure profile from the top wall of a plane \acrshort{tbl} was imposed on the top wall of the curved domain.
Since the pressure is the result of the flow behaviour, it cannot be set directly. Thus, an implicit approach was necessary to achieve the desired pressure distribution \citep{appelbaum_systematic_2025}.

This overview of current research about curved \acrshort{tbl} shows that there is an interest in investigating curvature effects on \acrshort{tbl}.
However, the separation of pressure and curvature effects is still an issue \citep{appelbaum_systematic_2025}.
Many investigations focus on rebuilding the pressure distribution of a curved domain on a flat domain to get some understanding.
These studies already created some knowledge about curved \acrshort{tbl}.
But  the effects of curvature and pressure gradient still interfere with each other.
The study of the isolated curvature in simulations is rare since the realisation of the \acrshort{zpg} is not straight-forward.
The objective of this project is to implement and evaluate a novel workflow for the configuration of a curved \acrshort{zpg} \acrshort{tbl}.
The project is founded on the preceding research from \citet{morita_applying_2022} who introduced an algorithm to achieve specific $\beta$-distributions.
The capability of the algorithm on curved domains was tested by \citet{ganagdhar_bayesian_2025}.
Nonetheless, the results are only partly transferable to the investigations from this study, since the curvature studied by \citet{ganagdhar_bayesian_2025} has a considerably  lower $\delta/R$ ratio. 
A detailed discussion about the transferability of other results is conducted in chapter XX.
The algorithm performs a shape optimisation of the upper wall in an \name{OpenFOAM} simulation with the objective of fitting a prescribed $\beta$ distribution at the lower wall.
In order to perform \acrshort{dns} simulations, it is necessary to transfer the result of the optimisation.
The present study investigates simulations based on the optimised \acrshort{dns}  domains utilising diverse curvature configurations.   
The proposed approach is based on \citet{schlatter_leveraging_nodate}'s work and will be presented in detail in \autoref{chap:methods}.

\section{Simulation techniques} 
\label{sec:SimBasics}

For the simulation of turbulent flows a range of methodologies can be employed.
The decision regarding the utilisation of a specific approach  depends on the characteristics of the flow and the requirements for the solution.
One straight-forward approach is the \acrshort{dns}.
\acrshort{dns} necessitates the solution of the \acrshort{nse} in order to compute  $U(x,t)$.
Thus, \acrshort{dns} is the computationally most expensive approach, given that it resolves all length and time scales \citep{pope_turbulent_2015}.  Conversely, this approach yields  the most accurate results.
In the context of small turbulent scales, the utilisation  of \acrshort{dns} is inevitable.
Due to the high computational demands, \acrshort{dns} is typically employed in the study of low to moderate Reynolds numbers. Its application is primarily restricted to research context \citep{slotnick_cfd_nodate}.
The simulation method of \acrshort{dns} is the principal approach employed in this project.
Moreover, we employed \acrshort{rans} simulations to ensure that the computational effort remained within acceptable limits.
%In this approach\fximprove{Meinst Du deinen eigenen Ansatz?} the \acrshort{rans} are solved instead of the full \acrshort{nse}.
As a consequence, only the mean velocity field is calculated.
The effect of turbulence can be summarised in the Reynolds stress tensor.
In order to circumvent the computation of this tensor, a number of methodologies have been formulated for its modelling \citep{wilcox_turbulence_2006}. 
Since the models have different characteristics and accuracies, the selection of the model will have a substantial impact on the simulation \citep{yang_assessment_2016}.
It is evident that these simulations show a considerably better computational performance.
In case the precise behaviour of the turbulent scales is irrelevant, and the main interest is the effect of the turbulence on the flow, \acrshort{rans} simulations provide a reasonable compromise \citep{alfonsi_reynolds-averaged_2009}.
Therefore, this simulation method finds application in multiple industrial environments.

\section{Tools}

Developing this project involved the utilisation of a range of tools. The majority of fluid-flow simulations were performed with DNS (see XX \autoref{sec:SimBasics}). To execute these simulations the spectral element code \name{neko} was used. \fxchange{Blabla über neko}

When running simulations with \name{neko}, it is possible to track statistics regarding the  simulated flow.  The recording of the statistics can be configured in various manners and is defined in the so-called case file of the simulation.
 For instance, it is possible to define one or two axes along which the statistics should be averaged. 
 These statistics are used to extract and calculate certain metrics of the flow. In order to accomplish this, it is necessary to undertake a process of post-processing of the recorded statistics. For this purpose,  the \name{python} package \texttt{pysemtools} is employed. 
 \name{pysemtools} facilitates the extraction and interpolation of data, like those resulting from  \name{neko} simulations,  from an SEM mesh into any arbitrary points \fxchange{ Quelle:GitHub repository}. Subsequently, a multitude of statistical metrics, including budget terms, can be calculated. 
 
In addition to the DNS,  a number of \acrshort{rans} simulations were conducted as well. In order to execute these simulations, the open-source software \name{OpenFOAM} is utilised. This software is able to perform \acrshort{rans} simulations of fluid flows with a variety of turbulence models. To extract velocity fields and related data from the simulation \name{Python} is used.
